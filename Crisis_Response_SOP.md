## Purpose & Scope of This Document

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) defines how TTAO Inner Academy responds to student psychological crises, elevated concerns, and safety situations. It establishes clear protocols for:

1. **Immediate crisis response** when a student expresses suicidal ideation, self-harm intent, or acute psychological distress
2. **Elevated concern monitoring** for students showing sustained distress patterns without acute crisis
3. **Crisis pause and re-entry procedures** for students resuming academy work after a crisis
4. **Access revocation** for students who repeatedly refuse safety protocols or pose risk to others
5. **Mandatory reporting** in cases of child abuse or vulnerable adult abuse disclosure
6. **International crisis resource matching** based on student location and language
7. **Documentation and legal compliance** for all crisis interventions

**This SOP is binding on all academy personnel (Headmaster and Guides) and must be followed in all crisis situations.**

---

## Boundaries: What the Academy Does and Does Not Do

### The Academy DOES

✅ Provide immediate acknowledgment and compassionate response to students in crisis  
✅ Direct students to appropriate crisis resources (crisis lines, emergency services, therapists)  
✅ Pause academy participation temporarily to protect student well-being  
✅ Offer low-pressure check-ins for students showing sustained distress  
✅ Support re-entry after crisis with modified pacing and one-on-one guidance  
✅ Document all crisis interactions for legal and operational purposes  
✅ Maintain confidentiality except where legally required to break it (mandatory reporting, imminent danger)

### The Academy DOES NOT

❌ Provide therapy, crisis counseling, or clinical mental health treatment  
❌ Diagnose mental health conditions or prescribe medication  
❌ Conduct suicide risk assessments or safety planning (refer to professionals)  
❌ Guarantee student safety or take responsibility for student actions outside the academy  
❌ Contact emergency services on behalf of students unless explicitly requested and legally appropriate  
❌ Serve as a substitute for professional mental health care  
❌ Retain students whose participation poses ongoing risk to themselves or others

**The Headmaster and Guides are educators, not licensed mental health professionals.** All crisis response is limited to acknowledgment, resource provision, and academy participation management.

## Part I: Immediate Crisis Response Protocol

### Trigger Conditions

This protocol is activated immediately when a student:

- Expresses **active suicidal ideation** (thoughts of ending their life with intent or plan)
- States intent to **self-harm** or describes recent self-harm behavior
- Describes **acute psychological crisis** (severe dissociation, psychotic break, panic attack lasting hours, etc.)
- Sends a message indicating they are **in immediate danger** to themselves or others

**Response Time:** Within 1 hour during waking hours (8 AM – 11 PM in Headmaster's timezone). If message received overnight, respond first thing in the morning with crisis resources and acknowledgment.

---

### Step 1: Acknowledge Immediately

Respond to the student's message with calm, non-judgmental acknowledgment. Do not attempt to "fix" the crisis or provide therapeutic intervention.

**Example Response:**

> "\[Student Name], I've received your message and I want you to know that I'm here and I hear you. What you're going through sounds incredibly difficult, and I'm glad you reached out. Your safety is the most important thing right now."

**Do NOT:**

- Minimize their distress ("It's not that bad," "You'll be fine")
- Offer platitudes ("Everything happens for a reason")
- Attempt to talk them out of their feelings
- Promise outcomes you cannot guarantee ("You're going to be okay")

---

### Step 2: Provide Crisis Resources Immediately

Direct the student to appropriate crisis support based on their country of residence (from intake form). Provide specific phone numbers and websites.

**Example Response:**

> "I want to make sure you have immediate support available. Please reach out to \[Crisis Line Name] at \[Phone Number] or visit \[Website]. They are trained crisis counselors available 24/7 who can provide the support you need right now.
>
> If you are in immediate physical danger or have a plan to harm yourself, please call emergency services at \[Emergency Number] or go to your nearest emergency room."

**Use the International Crisis Resources table (Part VII) to match the student's location.**

---

### Step 3: Ask One Grounding Question

After providing crisis resources, ask a single, simple grounding question to assess the student's immediate state. This is NOT a risk assessment—it is a gentle check-in.

**Example Questions:**

- "Are you in a safe place right now?"
- "Is there someone with you, or someone you can call?"
- "Have you been able to reach out to a crisis line or therapist?"

**Do NOT:**

- Ask multiple questions in rapid succession
- Demand detailed explanations of their crisis
- Conduct a formal suicide risk assessment (you are not qualified)

---

### Step 4: Pause Academy Participation

Inform the student that their academy participation is temporarily paused to prioritize their well-being. Frame this as supportive, not punitive.

**Example Response:**

> "I'm going to pause your access to the academy portal for now so you can focus entirely on your safety and getting the support you need. This is not a punishment or a permanent removal—it's a temporary pause to make sure you're taking care of yourself first. The academy will be here when you're ready to return."

**Technical Action:** Headmaster temporarily disables the student's portal access (or marks their account as "Crisis Pause" if full revocation is not possible).

**Important:** If the student repeatedly refuses to seek professional support, threatens other students, or uses the academy platform to escalate their crisis (e.g., posting graphic self-harm content in shared spaces), proceed to **Part V: Access Revocation for Non-Compliance**.

---

### Step 5: Document the Interaction

Immediately after the exchange, the Headmaster must document:

1. Date and time of student's crisis message
2. Content of student's message (verbatim if possible, or detailed summary)
3. Headmaster's response (verbatim)
4. Crisis resources provided (specific line and country)
5. Student's response to grounding question (if any)
6. Action taken (portal access paused, follow-up scheduled, etc.)

**Use the Documentation Template in Part IX.**

**Retention:** Crisis documentation is retained for **7 years** following the student's withdrawal or completion, in accordance with standard educational record-keeping practices.

---

### Step 5A: Emergency Services Escalation (Rare Cases Only)

**When to Contact Emergency Services:**

The academy does NOT routinely contact emergency services on behalf of students. However, in the following rare circumstances, the Headmaster may contact emergency services:

1. **Student explicitly requests emergency services contact** and provides their location and consent
2. **Student is a minor (under 18)** and expresses active suicidal intent with a plan
3. **Student discloses imminent harm to another person** (e.g., "I'm going to hurt \[specific person] tonight")

**Procedure:**

1. Confirm the student's location (address, city, country)
2. Call the appropriate emergency number for their country (911, 999, 112, 000, etc.)
3. Provide dispatcher with: student's name, location, nature of crisis, and your role as academy administrator
4. Document the call immediately (time, dispatcher name if available, outcome)
5. Notify the student that emergency services have been contacted (if safe to do so)

**Legal Note:** In most jurisdictions, educators and online program administrators are NOT mandatory reporters for adult suicidal ideation unless there is imminent, specific danger. Consult local laws if uncertain. When in doubt, prioritize student safety and document your reasoning.

## Part II: Student Follows Up or Does Not Respond

### Scenario A: Student Responds Positively

If the student responds within 24–48 hours and indicates they have:

- Contacted a crisis line or therapist
- Sought support from family/friends
- Stabilized and no longer in acute crisis

**Headmaster Action:**

1. Acknowledge their follow-up with warmth and encouragement
2. Ask if they would like to schedule a one-on-one check-in session (optional, not required)
3. Inform them that portal access will remain paused until they explicitly request re-entry (see **Part IV: Re-Entry Protocol**)
4. Document the follow-up

**Example Response:**

> "Thank you for letting me know you're safe and getting support. I'm really glad to hear that. There's no rush to return to the academy—take all the time you need. When you feel ready to resume your work, just let me know and we'll schedule a brief check-in session before restoring your access. You're welcome back whenever you're ready."

---

### Scenario B: Student Does Not Respond

If the student does not respond within **48 hours** of the initial crisis message:

**Headmaster Action:**

1. Send one follow-up message expressing concern and reiterating crisis resources
2. Do NOT send multiple messages or attempt to "chase" the student
3. Document the lack of response
4. Portal access remains paused indefinitely until student initiates contact

**Example Follow-Up Message:**

> "\[Student Name], I haven't heard back from you and I'm thinking of you. I hope you're safe and getting the support you need. If you're able, please let me know you're okay—even just a brief message. If you need crisis support, \[Crisis Line] is available 24/7 at \[Number]. The academy will be here whenever you're ready to return. Take care of yourself."

**Do NOT:**

- Contact the student's emergency contact (if provided) unless the student is a minor or has explicitly consented to emergency contact notification
- Assume the worst or escalate unnecessarily
- Remove the student from the academy roster—they remain enrolled but paused

**If the student does not respond for 30 days:** Send one final check-in message. If no response after 60 days, the Headmaster may administratively withdraw the student with a note that they are welcome to reapply when ready.

## Part III: Elevated Concerns (Non-Acute Patterns)

### Trigger Conditions for Non-Acute Patterns

This protocol applies when a student is NOT in acute crisis, but the Headmaster or Guide observes sustained patterns of distress, such as:

- **Prolonged silence** (no portal activity, no journal entries, no responses to messages for 2+ weeks)
- **Disconnected or fragmented journal entries** that suggest dissociation or cognitive disorganization
- **Repeated expressions of hopelessness, worthlessness, or despair** without active suicidal ideation
- **Withdrawal from community** (stops attending group sessions, stops responding to peers)
- **Escalating distress over multiple weeks** (e.g., student reports worsening anxiety, nightmares, or intrusive thoughts)

**Key Distinction:** The student is NOT in immediate danger, but their well-being is a concern.

---

### Response Protocol

#### Step 1: Low-Pressure Check-In

The Headmaster or assigned Guide sends a brief, non-intrusive message expressing care and offering support.

**Example Message:**

> "Hi \[Student Name], I've noticed you've been quieter in the portal lately, and I wanted to check in. There's no pressure to respond if you're not up for it—I just want you to know I'm here if you need anything. If you'd like to talk, I'm happy to schedule a one-on-one session. If you need some space, that's completely okay too. Take care."

**Do NOT:**

- Demand explanations for their absence
- Assume the worst or catastrophize
- Pressure them to "catch up" on academy work

---

#### Step 2: Offer Inner Work Pause (Optional)

If the student responds and indicates they are struggling, offer the option to pause inner work temporarily while continuing to access the portal for community and reading.

**Example Response:**

> "It sounds like things are really heavy right now. Would it help to pause the inner work practices for a bit and just focus on the reading and community? You can still access the portal and participate in discussions without the pressure of journaling or visualization work. Let me know what feels right for you."

---

#### Step 3: Recommend External Support

If the student describes ongoing distress, gently recommend they seek support from a therapist or counselor.

**Example Response:**

> "It sounds like you're carrying a lot right now. Have you considered talking to a therapist or counselor? Sometimes having that outside support can make a big difference, especially when doing deep inner work. I'm not saying you have to—just offering it as an option. The academy will support you however we can, but a professional might be able to help in ways we can't."

**Do NOT:**

- Diagnose the student or suggest specific treatments
- Insist they see a therapist as a condition of continued enrollment (unless they meet contraindication criteria from intake)

---

#### Step 4: Document the Check-In

Record the date, nature of concern, message sent, student's response (if any), and any actions taken (e.g., inner work paused, external support recommended).

## Part IV: Crisis Pause & Re-Entry Protocol

### When a Student Requests to Return

After a crisis pause, the student must initiate contact to request re-entry. The academy does NOT automatically restore access after a set period—the student must affirmatively indicate readiness.

---

### Step 1: Acknowledge the Request

Respond warmly and without judgment.

**Example Response:**

> "I'm really glad to hear from you, and I'm glad you're feeling ready to return. Before we restore your portal access, I'd like to schedule a brief one-on-one session (about 30 minutes) just to check in and make sure we're supporting you in the right way. Does that work for you?"

---

### Step 2: Conduct One-on-One Re-Entry Session

The Headmaster or assigned Guide conducts a video or voice call (not text-only) to assess the student's readiness. This is NOT a clinical assessment—it is a supportive check-in.

**Questions to Ask:**

1. "How are you feeling now compared to when we paused your access?"
2. "Have you been able to get support from a therapist, crisis line, or other resources?"
3. "What made you feel ready to return to the academy?"
4. "Is there anything you need from me or the academy to make your return feel safe and manageable?"

**Red Flags (Do NOT restore access yet):**

- Student is still in acute crisis or expresses active suicidal ideation
- Student has not sought any external support and is relying solely on the academy for mental health needs
- Student is hostile, defensive, or refuses to discuss their well-being

**Green Lights (Proceed with re-entry):**

- Student reports stabilization and improved well-being
- Student has engaged with external support (therapist, crisis line, trusted friend/family)
- Student expresses realistic understanding of their capacity and boundaries

---

### Step 3: Restore Access with Modified Re-Entry Plan

If the student is ready, restore portal access and recommend a modified re-entry plan:

**Re-Entry Recommendations:**

- **Start with foundational work:** Review Appendix E.4 (Grounding & Integration Protocols) before resuming inner work
- **Reduce intensity:** Pause advanced practices (e.g., deep trance, shadow work) and focus on gentler techniques (e.g., breath work, simple visualization)
- **Increase check-ins:** Schedule weekly or bi-weekly one-on-one sessions for the first month
- **Set boundaries:** Encourage the student to honor Appendix E.5 (When to Pause Inner Work) and communicate early if distress re-emerges

**Example Message:**

> "I'm restoring your portal access now. I'd recommend starting with the grounding practices in Appendix E.4 and taking things slowly for the first few weeks. Let's plan to check in weekly for the next month so I can support you as you ease back in. If at any point things feel overwhelming, please let me know right away—we can always pause again. There's no penalty for taking care of yourself."

---

### Step 4: Document the Re-Entry

Record the date of re-entry session, student's reported status, re-entry plan, and any ongoing support measures.

---

### No Stigma, No Penalty

**Students who pause due to crisis are NOT penalized in any way.** They do not lose their enrollment, their tier status, or their standing in the academy. Crisis pauses are confidential and are not disclosed to other students.

## Part V: Access Revocation for Non-Compliance

### When Access Revocation May Be Necessary

In rare cases, a student's behavior may pose ongoing risk to themselves, other students, or the integrity of the academy. Access revocation is a last resort and is only used when:

1. **Student repeatedly refuses safety protocols** after multiple warnings (e.g., continues advanced inner work despite contraindications, ignores grounding protocols, refuses to seek external support during ongoing crisis)
2. **Student threatens or harasses other students** (e.g., sends threatening messages, posts graphic self-harm content in shared spaces, engages in predatory behavior)
3. **Student uses the academy platform to escalate their crisis** (e.g., repeatedly posts suicidal content in public forums despite redirection, uses the academy as a substitute for professional mental health care)
4. **Student engages in illegal activity** (e.g., shares explicit content involving minors, threatens violence, engages in fraud)

---

### Step 1: Issue One Clear Written Warning

Before revoking access, the Headmaster must issue one formal written warning that:

1. Describes the specific behavior of concern
2. Explains why the behavior violates academy safety protocols or community standards
3. States the required corrective action (e.g., "You must seek support from a licensed therapist before resuming inner work")
4. Warns that continued non-compliance will result in access revocation
5. Provides a deadline for corrective action (typically 7–14 days)

**Example Warning:**

> "\[Student Name],
>
> I'm writing to address a serious concern about your participation in the academy. Over the past \[timeframe], you have \[specific behavior: e.g., continued advanced shadow work despite multiple recommendations to pause, posted graphic descriptions of self-harm in the shared journal space, refused to seek external support despite ongoing suicidal ideation].
>
> This behavior violates the academy's safety protocols (specifically Appendix E.5: When to Pause Inner Work) and puts your well-being at risk. It also affects the safety and well-being of other students in the community.
>
> **Required Action:** You must \[specific corrective action: e.g., schedule an appointment with a licensed therapist and provide confirmation of ongoing care, cease posting graphic content in shared spaces, agree to pause advanced inner work until cleared by a mental health professional].
>
> **Deadline:** Please complete this action by \[date, typically 7–14 days from warning].
>
> If you do not complete this action by the deadline, or if the concerning behavior continues, I will have no choice but to revoke your access to the academy. This is not a decision I take lightly, but the safety of all students—including you—is my responsibility.
>
> I'm here to support you, but I cannot do so if you are unwilling to take the steps necessary to protect your own well-being. Please let me know if you have questions or need help connecting with resources.
>
> \[Headmaster Name]"

**Document the warning immediately.**

---

### Step 2: Revoke Access if Non-Compliance Continues

If the student does not complete the required corrective action by the deadline, or if the concerning behavior continues, the Headmaster revokes portal access.

**Revocation Message:**

> "\[Student Name],
>
> I have not received confirmation that you completed \[required action], and/or the concerning behavior has continued. As stated in my previous message, I am now revoking your access to the TTAO Inner Academy portal, effective immediately.
>
> This decision is final. You are welcome to reapply to the academy in the future if your circumstances change and you are able to meet the academy's safety requirements. If you choose to reapply, you will need to provide \[specific requirement: e.g., written clearance from a licensed therapist, demonstration of 6 months of stable mental health care, etc.].
>
> I wish you well and hope you are able to get the support you need.
>
> \[Headmaster Name]"

**Technical Action:** Headmaster permanently disables portal access and removes the student from the active roster.

**Document the revocation immediately, including all prior warnings and communications.**

---

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Refunds:** The academy's refund policy (if any) applies. If the student has paid for a subscription period, consult the academy's terms of service regarding refunds for revoked access.
- **Confidentiality:** The reason for revocation is NOT disclosed to other students. If other students ask, the Headmaster may say only: "\[Student Name] is no longer enrolled in the academy."
- **No Public Shaming:** The student's name and situation are never discussed publicly or used as an example in academy communications.

## Part VI: Child or Vulnerable Adult Abuse Disclosure

### Mandatory Reporting Obligations

If a student discloses that they are currently experiencing abuse (physical, sexual, emotional, or neglect), the Headmaster's legal obligations depend on:

1. **The student's age** (minor vs. adult)
2. **The student's jurisdiction** (mandatory reporting laws vary by country and state/province)
3. **The Headmaster's jurisdiction** (where the Headmaster is located)

**General Guidance:**

- **If the student is under 18 (minor):** Most jurisdictions require educators and online program administrators to report suspected child abuse to local authorities. The Headmaster should consult local laws and, when in doubt, report.
- **If the student is an adult:** Mandatory reporting for adult abuse is less common, but some jurisdictions require reporting if the adult is a "vulnerable adult" (e.g., elderly, disabled, dependent on a caregiver). Consult local laws.

**The Headmaster is NOT required to investigate or verify the abuse.** The role is to report the disclosure to the appropriate authorities and let them investigate.

---

### Step 1: Listen and Document

If a student discloses abuse:

1. **Listen without judgment.** Do not interrogate or demand details.
2. **Thank them for trusting you.** Acknowledge their courage in sharing.
3. **Document the disclosure immediately:** Date, time, student's exact words (verbatim if possible), and any details provided (e.g., identity of abuser, location, frequency).

**Do NOT:**

- Promise confidentiality (you may be legally required to report)
- Confront the alleged abuser
- Attempt to investigate or verify the abuse yourself

---

### Step 2: Inform the Student of Reporting Obligations

If you are legally required to report (or believe you may be), inform the student immediately:

**Example Response:**

> "\[Student Name], thank you for trusting me with this. I want you to know that I believe you, and I'm here to support you. However, I need to let you know that because you are \[under 18 / a vulnerable adult], I am legally required to report this disclosure to \[appropriate authority: e.g., child protective services, adult protective services, local police]. This is not because I don't trust you—it's because the law requires me to make sure you're safe. I will not investigate or confront anyone—I will simply pass this information to the authorities so they can help."

---

### Step 3: Make the Report

Contact the appropriate authority based on the student's location:

- **United States (minors):** Call the Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline at 1-800-422-4453, or report to the student's local child protective services (CPS) agency. Directory: [childwelfare.gov/organizations](https://www.childwelfare.gov/organizations/)
- **United Kingdom (minors):** Call the NSPCC Helpline at 0808 800 5000, or report to the student's local council social services. [nspcc.org.uk](https://www.nspcc.org.uk)
- **Canada (minors):** Report to the student's provincial child welfare agency. Directory: [cwrp.ca](https://cwrp.ca)
- **Australia (minors):** Report to the student's state/territory child protection service. Directory: [aifs.gov.au](https://aifs.gov.au/resources/resource-sheets/mandatory-reporting-child-abuse-and-neglect)

**For adult abuse:** Consult local adult protective services or elder abuse hotlines.

**Document the report:** Date, time, agency contacted, name of intake worker (if available), and case number (if provided).

---

### Step 4: Follow Up with the Student

After making the report, inform the student that the report has been made and offer continued support:

**Example Message:**

> "I've made the report to \[agency name] as required. They will follow up with you directly. I want you to know that this doesn't change your standing in the academy—you are still welcome here, and I'm still here to support you. If you need to talk or if there's anything I can do to help you feel safe, please let me know."

**Do NOT:**

- Pressure the student to provide updates on the investigation
- Attempt to mediate between the student and the alleged abuser
- Assume the student will be removed from the abusive situation immediately (investigations take time)

## Part VIII: Language Barrier Support

### When a Student Does Not Speak English Fluently

If a student's primary language is not English and they are struggling to communicate during a crisis:

1. **Use translation tools:** Google Translate, DeepL, or other translation services can help bridge the gap. Acknowledge the limitation: "I'm using a translation tool to understand you—please let me know if anything I say is unclear."
2. **Provide crisis lines in their language:** Use the International Crisis Resources table (Part VII) to find crisis lines that operate in the student's language. The EU-wide 116 123 line offers multilingual support.
3. **Simplify language:** Use short, clear sentences. Avoid idioms, slang, or complex phrasing.
4. **Offer written communication:** If the student is more comfortable writing than speaking, allow them to communicate via text (email, portal message, Discord) rather than requiring a voice/video call.
5. **Do NOT assume comprehension:** Ask the student to confirm they understand key information (e.g., "Can you confirm you have the crisis line number saved?").

**Document language barriers and any translation tools used.**

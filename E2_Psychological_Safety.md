# TTAO Inner Academy

## Safety Document E.2 — Psychological Safety & Duty of Care

*This document applies to all inner work practices in the curriculum. Students read and acknowledge this document before beginning Year One Month 6. It applies with increasing weight through Years Two and Three as the depth of the inner work intensifies.*

---

### What This Document Is

The TTAO curriculum includes guided imaginal and inner work practices — principally the Desoille Directed Waking Dream sequences, which involve deliberate contact with subconscious material through a structured, meditative descent. These practices have a long tradition and can be genuinely transformative. They also carry real psychological risk if approached without appropriate awareness, preparation, or support.

This document is not a warning to avoid the work. It is a framework for doing it safely.

---

### The First Year as Preparation

Year One's inner work is specifically designed to build self-knowledge and inner stability *before* the more intensive practices are assigned. The daily meditation practice, the nature vigils, the journal discipline, the threefold breath, the inner flame, and the Declaration of Intent are not preliminary formalities — they are the foundation that makes the descent work safe.

Students who have genuinely established these practices across Year One will arrive at the deeper work from a measurably more grounded place. Do not shortcut the foundation. Do not advance to Month 6 inner work if the meditation practice has not been consistently established.

The instructor note at the end of Year One Month 1 applies throughout the curriculum: students who have not established the meditation practice before proceeding should hold at the earlier stage until the practice is genuinely underway.

---

### Contraindications — When Not to Proceed with Deep Descent Work

Do not engage in the descent practices (Desoille-based waking dream work and the deeper inner work of Years Two and Three) if any of the following apply:

- You are currently experiencing **acute psychosis, mania, or severe dissociation**.
- You have experienced a **significant traumatic event within the last six months** and have not yet begun processing it with professional support.
- You have a diagnosed **dissociative disorder (DID or OSDD)** without a currently active treating therapist.
- You have **untreated panic disorder** that has triggered during meditation, closed-eye visualisation, or similar practices.
- You are currently in an **acute mental health crisis** of any kind.
- You are experiencing **active suicidal ideation**.

If you identify with any of the above, speak with the headmaster or your guide before Month 6. You are not excluded from the academy — you may continue with foundational laboratory work and the meditation practices while addressing the above with appropriate support. The inner work will be waiting when you are ready.

If you are unsure whether a condition applies to you, err on the side of caution and consult a healthcare professional.

---

### Recognising When Something Has Gone Wrong

The descent practices are designed to bring latent material to the surface. Some discomfort, unsettling imagery, or unfamiliar emotion is normal and expected — this is part of the Work. The following, however, are signals to stop and seek support:

- Prolonged distress that does not resolve within 24 hours after a session
- Inability to return to ordinary awareness after a session ends
- Intrusive imagery that persists into waking daily life without abating
- Significant dissociation: feeling unreal, detached from your body, or unable to distinguish inner experience from outer reality
- Obsessive engagement with the inner material — inability to step back from it
- A strong feeling that the inner figures or imagery are communicating something that others cannot perceive or would not accept

These are not signs of failure or weakness. They are signals that the practice has moved faster or deeper than the current support structure can safely hold. The appropriate response is to stop, ground, and reach out.

---

### Grounding After a Difficult Session

If a session produces distress or difficulty returning to ordinary awareness:

1. **Stop the practice immediately.** Do not attempt to continue or "push through."
2. **Ground physically.** Press both feet flat on the floor. Feel the weight of the chair beneath you. Look at three objects in the room and name them aloud. Pick up something with texture — a stone, a piece of wood — and hold it.
3. **Breathe.** Three slow, deliberate breaths with a full exhale. Return to the sensation of the body.
4. **Do something ordinary.** Drink water. Wash your hands. Go outside briefly if possible.
5. **Write in the journal.** A brief, factual account of what happened. This externalises the material and begins to restore the boundary between inner and outer.
6. **Do not attempt another session the same day.**

---

### The Referral Pathway

If grounding is insufficient, or if distress persists, use this pathway in order:

**Step 1 — Contact your TTAO guide or the headmaster.** Do not continue the practice alone until you have spoken with a guide. The headmaster is available for personal one-on-one sessions. Use the academy's messaging system to reach out; a response will be prioritised.

Your guide will help you ground, assess whether the practice should be paused, and, if appropriate, refer you to professional resources. **Guides do not provide psychotherapy.**

**Step 2 — Inform your therapist, if you have one.** Describe what occurred and ask for support. You may provide them with the Desoille source texts (*DES-1, DES-2, DES-3*) as reference for the practice. General psychotherapy is appropriate even if your therapist is unfamiliar with the Desoille method specifically — any practitioner familiar with guided imagery, EMDR, somatic trauma approaches, or inner child work will be able to assist.

**Step 3 — If you do not have a therapist and the distress is significant,** seek a trauma-informed therapist. Use your country's professional association directory or ask your GP for a referral. If you are unsure what to look for, ask the headmaster or guide for assistance finding appropriate local support.

**Step 4 — If you are in crisis** — if you are experiencing suicidal ideation, severe dissociation, or a psychiatric emergency — contact your local emergency mental health service or crisis line immediately. In the UK: call 116 123 (Samaritans) or 999. In the US: call or text 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline). In other countries: seek your national crisis line. The academy supports your safety above all else.

### Supplementary Self-Regulation Tools

The following tools are available for working with identified emotional blockages during the course of the inner work. They are supplementary tools — not replacements for professional support when deeper destabilisation occurs.

**EFT Tapping (Emotional Freedom Technique):** Tapping specific meridian points while speaking to a feeling or belief. Useful for processing discrete, identifiable emotional charges. The *EFT International Free Tapping Manual* is assigned in Year One Month 6. Protocol: identify the feeling precisely; tap the sequence (crown, brow, beside eye, under eye, under nose, chin, collarbone, under arm, crown) while speaking to the feeling; repeat until the charge diminishes. Record the session.

**The Sedona Method:** A structured inquiry for releasing held emotional states by identifying, allowing, and releasing feelings rather than processing or analysing them. Useful in conjunction with EFT and as a standalone tool in periods of emotional turbulence. Students interested in the Sedona Method are encouraged to obtain *The Sedona Method* (Dwoskin) as supplementary reading.

**Somatic grounding:** The body-awareness practices introduced in Year Two Month 8 — attending to sensation without judgment, the felt sense of weight and temperature, breath tracking — are effective stabilising practices available at any time.

Neither EFT nor the Sedona Method should be applied to acute trauma, crisis states, or dissociative episodes. In those cases, use the referral pathway above.

---

### The Role of the Guide in Inner Work Sessions

TTAO guides are required to have personally undergone the Desoille method and to have a thorough working knowledge of the three Desoille texts. A guide's role in relation to inner work is:

- To confirm the student is meeting the foundational prerequisites before advancing to deeper practices
- To review journal accounts of descent sessions and identify signs of difficulty
- To respond promptly when a student reports distress from inner work
- To direct students to appropriate external referral when needed
- **Not** to serve as a therapist or crisis counsellor — guides refer when the situation exceeds the scope of peer support

---

### A Note on Sincerity

The alchemical tradition has always recognised that the inner work is not merely meditation — it is the transformation of the practitioner. The practices in this curriculum are assigned because the tradition holds, and experience confirms, that outer laboratory work and inner transformation proceed together or not at all.

That seriousness is precisely why this document exists. The Work is real. Its effects are real. Appropriate care is not a bureaucratic formality — it is part of the tradition's own demand for honesty, self-knowledge, and genuine preparation before proceeding.

### Fire & Heat

- **Never** leave an active heating source unattended.
- Maintain a fire extinguisher (CO₂ or dry chemical) within arm's reach of the workstation at all times.
- Do not heat sealed vessels without a pressure-relief provision.
- Use heat-resistant gloves when handling hot glassware or crucibles.
- The water bath (Balneum Mariae) is the first degree of fire and is sufficient for most Year One operations. Do not advance to higher degrees without guide confirmation of readiness.
- Keep a dedicated bucket of clean sand beside the workstation for smothering small fires in open vessels (preferable to water for flammable-liquid fires).

### Alcohol & Flammable Menstrua

- High-proof spirits of wine are flammable. Keep them away from open flame during use, especially while pouring.
- Store all proof spirits in sealed dark glass bottles, away from heat sources and direct sunlight.
- Do not store more than one litre of high-proof alcohol in a home workspace without a proper flameproof storage cabinet.
- The **Cotton Wick Test** (Year One, Month 3) must be passed before any alcohol is used as a menstruum. This requirement is non-negotiable.
- Legal restrictions on the distillation and possession of high-proof spirits vary by jurisdiction. Students are **solely responsible** for understanding and complying with their local laws before obtaining or producing rectified spirits of wine. **The academy disclaims any and all liability for legal non-compliance, accidents, or damages arising from the use or storage of flammable menstrua.**

### Glassware

- Use only **borosilicate (Pyrex-type) glass** for all heating operations. Soda-lime glass is not appropriate for laboratory heat work and will crack unpredictably.
- Inspect all glassware for cracks, star fractures, chips, or thin spots before each session. Discard compromised pieces — do not attempt to repair cracked flasks.
- Never apply sudden heat to cold glassware. Warm gradually from room temperature.
- Do not set hot glassware on a cold or wet surface.
- **If commercial borosilicate is unavailable:** clay and earthenware calcining vessels are acceptable alternatives for calcination work. Academy tutorials on constructing clay vessels are available. Knowing or collaborating with a glassblower is a significant practical advantage — attending a glassblowing workshop is recommended where accessible.
- Label every vessel in permanent marker before filling: contents, date, and tier of preparation.

### Ventilation

- **All calcination must be performed either outdoors or with direct ventilation to the outside.** Burning plant matter produces carbon monoxide and fine particulates. A window fan pointed outward is the minimum acceptable arrangement; dedicated fume extraction is preferred.
- All long digestions and distillations should be conducted in well-ventilated spaces. If you cannot smell fresh air in your workspace, it is insufficiently ventilated.
- If a preparation produces an unexpected or very pungent smell, move the operation outside immediately and allow the space to air before re-entering.

**Disclaimer:** These are general ventilation guidelines. Students are solely responsible for ensuring their workspace has adequate and safe ventilation for all operations, and for verifying its effectiveness. The academy disclaims any liability for inadequate ventilation or related health issues.

### Personal Protective Equipment — Minimum Requirements

| Tier | Required PPE |
| ---- | ------------ |
| Year One | Safety glasses or goggles (whenever heat is in use); heat-resistant gloves (for calcination); dedicated lab clothes or apron |
| Year Two | All of Year One; nitrile chemical-resistant gloves (for any solvent or mineral salt work) |
| Year Three | All of Year Two; full-coverage eye protection (chemical splash goggles, not merely glasses); acid-resistant gloves for corrosive solvent work *(see E.3)* |

Safety glasses are not optional during any operation involving heat, alcohol, or corrosive materials. The cost of a pair of safety glasses is trivial; the cost of an eye injury is not.

### Record-Keeping as Safety Practice

Every laboratory session must be logged in the alchemical journal:

- Date and time
- Materials used (substance, source, quantity)
- Procedure performed
- Observations (including unexpected occurrences, colour changes, smells, or anomalies)
- Any safety concerns noted

This record serves a dual purpose: it is the student's primary progress documentation and their primary safety log. A preparation that goes wrong can be analysed only if its history is recorded. The journal is the alchemist's most important safety tool after their own attention.

---

### Emergency Procedures

**Fire:** Smother with the sand bucket if small and contained. Use the CO₂ extinguisher if spreading. Evacuate and call emergency services if not immediately controllable.

**Alcohol spill with ignition:** Do not use water. Smother with sand or CO₂. Remove all ignition sources from the area before cleaning up unignited spills.

**Glass cut:** Clean, apply pressure, seek medical attention if deep. Do not continue laboratory work with an unprotected open wound.

**Overheating preparation:** Do not open the vessel. Remove from heat source. Allow to cool before handling. Assess seal integrity before resuming.

**Persistent unusual smell:** Cease the operation, ventilate the space, and consult your laboratory notes and guide before resuming.

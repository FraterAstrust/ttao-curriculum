### Overview

Mineral work in Year Three introduces hazardous materials not present in vegetable or water work. The principal hazard is **antimony** — specifically stibnite (antimony trisulfide), the Grey Wolf of the alchemical tradition. A secondary category of hazard is corrosive solvents used in advanced Via Humida work.

These materials are genuinely hazardous when mishandled. They are also manageable with appropriate precautions. The following requirements are not suggestions. Entry into mineral work is conditional on meeting them.

---

### Antimony: What You Are Working With

**Disclaimer:** The information provided below is for educational awareness of the materials you *may choose* to work with. It does not constitute a comprehensive safety data sheet, chemical hazard assessment, or professional handling instructions. **Students are SOLELY responsible for independently researching, understanding, and safely handling all substances, and for all health and safety implications.**

**Stibnite** (Sb₂S₃) is a natural antimony sulfide mineral, grey-black in colour and often crystalline. It is the primary starting material for alchemical antimony work.

**Antimony trioxide** (Sb₂O₃) — produced when stibnite is roasted or heated in air — is the primary hazardous compound generated during calcination. It is classified as a **possible human carcinogen** (Group 2B, IARC). Inhalation of antimony trioxide dust or fumes is the primary route of significant exposure. **The academy disclaims any responsibility for acute or chronic health effects resulting from student exposure to antimony compounds.**

**Antimony trichloride** (*Butyrum Antimonii*) is produced in advanced Year Three preparations. It is **corrosive and toxic** and must be handled only with full PPE as described below.

Antimony compounds accumulate in the body over time. Chronic low-level exposure is a greater concern than a single accidental exposure. Good practice every session matters more than heroic caution on one occasion.

### Ventilation Requirements

**This is the most important requirement. It cannot be substituted or bypassed.**

**Disclaimer:** These are general educational guidelines for ventilation. **Students are SOLELY responsible for designing, implementing, and verifying the effectiveness of their ventilation setup** to ensure safe operation with hazardous mineral compounds. The academy provides educational information but disclaims all liability for any health issues, accidents, or regulatory non-compliance arising from inadequate ventilation.

All heating of antimony ore or compounds — including initial roasting, calcination at any temperature, and distillation involving antimony — must be performed under one of the following conditions:

- **Fully outdoors**, with the student positioned upwind of the preparation
- **In a dedicated fume hood** venting directly to the exterior of the building
- **At an open window** with a powerful exhaust fan drawing air from the workspace directly outside, with the student positioned between the fresh air intake and the preparation

A domestic kitchen or bathroom with the window open is not adequate for antimony calcination. An open garage with cross-ventilation may be acceptable for initial small-scale work if prevailing wind conditions are favourable — confirm with your guide.

**Do not rely on smell as a safety indicator for antimony fumes.** Antimony trioxide has a low odour threshold but the absence of smell does not mean safe levels. Always use ventilation regardless of whether you can detect an odour.

### Personal Protective Equipment — Year Three Requirements

| Situation | Minimum PPE |
| --------- | ----------- |
| Handling raw stibnite (no heat) | Nitrile gloves, safety glasses |
| Calcining antimony compounds | Nitrile gloves, chemical splash goggles (not glasses), N95 respirator minimum; P100 half-face respirator strongly preferred |
| Working with corrosive solvents (*Butyrum* preparations, mineral acid work) | Acid-resistant gloves (butyl or neoprene, not nitrile alone), chemical splash goggles, lab coat or dedicated long-sleeved clothing covering all skin |
| Any antimony work involving heat | All of the above plus heat-resistant outer gloves over chemical gloves where feasible |

**An N95 mask is the minimum for any heated antimony work.** A P100 half-face respirator is strongly recommended. Standard dust masks and surgical masks are not adequate protection against antimony oxide particulate at operating temperatures.

### Handling and Work Practices

- **Never handle antimony materials without gloves**, even cold and apparently inert. Antimony is absorbed through skin over time.
- Wash hands and forearms thoroughly after every antimony session — even if gloves were worn throughout.
- **Do not eat, drink, or touch your face** during or immediately after any antimony work session.
- Do not work with antimony when fatigued, distracted, or unwell.
- Keep antimony work sessions to a reasonable duration. Shorter, more attentive sessions are safer than long, habituated ones.
- Work with the smallest quantity of material that will accomplish the preparation. There is no alchemical benefit to working with larger amounts of hazardous material than necessary.
- Weigh and record all quantities in the laboratory journal before beginning any session.

---

### Storage

- Store all antimony materials in **sealed, clearly labelled containers**, separate from all other laboratory materials.
- Store containers in a location that cannot be accessed by children, animals, or household members who have not received this safety briefing.
- Store well away from food preparation and eating areas — not in the kitchen, not near the refrigerator.
- Do not store antimony near strong acids or oxidising agents.
- Label containers with: contents, date acquired, source, and a clear hazard indicator (e.g., "ANTIMONY — TOXIC — DO NOT TOUCH").

### Disposal

Antimony waste — spent ore, calcined ash, used solutions, and contaminated materials — must be disposed of as **hazardous waste**. Do not pour antimony-containing liquids down the drain. Do not dispose of solid antimony waste in ordinary household rubbish.

Contact your local council, county, or municipality for hazardous household waste collection information. Many areas have periodic collection days or designated drop-off facilities for hazardous household chemicals. If in doubt, contact your guide or headmaster for guidance specific to your region.

### Corrosive Solvents: Additional Precautions

Advanced Via Humida preparations in Year Three may involve strong mineral acids or highly concentrated salt solutions as solvents.

**Disclaimer:** These are extremely hazardous materials. **Students are SOLELY responsible for their safe handling, storage, emergency response, and for understanding the specific hazards of each chemical.** The academy disclaims all liability for any injuries, damages, or health issues arising from the student's use of corrosive solvents.

Before working with any corrosive reagent:

1. Research the material fully before purchase or use. Know its hazard classification, appropriate PPE, and emergency treatment before it arrives in your workspace.
2. Never mix reagents without understanding the reaction. Do not combine unknown materials.
3. Have **running water immediately accessible** during all corrosive solvent work for immediate rinsing of skin or eyes.
4. If acid contacts skin: rinse immediately with copious running water for at least 15 minutes. Seek medical attention if burning persists.
5. If acid contacts eyes: rinse immediately with running water, holding the eye open, for at least 15 minutes. Seek emergency medical attention.
6. Inform your guide before beginning any corrosive solvent work and confirm your workspace meets all requirements above.

### Year Three Safety Quiz

Every student must pass a safety assessment before beginning antimony or corrosive solvent work. The quiz covers:

1. Antimony toxicity pathways (inhalation, skin absorption, ingestion)
2. Correct ventilation setup for your specific workspace
3. Required PPE for calcination, handling, and corrosive work
4. Emergency responses: inhalation, skin contact, eye contact, spill, fire
5. Storage and labelling requirements
6. Disposal regulations for your jurisdiction

The quiz is administered by the headmaster or a designated guide and may be completed via written response, video session, or live conversation. A student who cannot correctly answer the core questions does not begin mineral work until the relevant knowledge is confirmed.

This is not bureaucracy. This is the tradition's demand that the practitioner know their materials before working with them — applied to the specific conditions of modern hazardous materials.

### A Note on the Tradition

The alchemical tradition worked with antimony without modern understanding of its toxicology. Many historical practitioners suffered accordingly. The tradition's demand for careful, deliberate, patient attention to what is in the vessel — and to what is in oneself — is also a demand for attention to the physical realities of the work. A practitioner who damages their health through careless mineral work has not practiced the Work: they have practiced impatience with dangerous materials.

The Grey Wolf is a teacher. Part of what it teaches is respect for the nature of the mineral kingdom, which does not forgive inattention as readily as the vegetable kingdom does.

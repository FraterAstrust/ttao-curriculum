### Overview

Year Two introduces the Via Humida — the wet path of vegetable alchemy and the extended circulation processes of Pelican work. Several preparations in this path involve materials that are biologically active: living or decomposing plant matter, outdoor dews, rainwater, and extended putrefaction (the Black Stage, or Nigredo, in wet processing). These materials present biological hazards distinct from the chemical hazards addressed in Safety Documents E.1 and E.3.

This document covers:
- Dew collection (Gur) and rainwater collection
- Putrefaction processes in sealed vessels
- Extended maceration and circulating digestions
- General hygiene protocols for wet path work
- Cross-reference to laboratory safety

---

### Understanding the Biological Hazard

Plant matter in the process of putrefaction supports microbial growth — bacteria, moulds, and yeasts. Under normal conditions, healthy adults handle everyday microbial exposure without difficulty. However, certain aspects of traditional wet path work concentrate or enclose this biological activity in ways that warrant deliberate precaution:

- **Sealed vessel putrefaction** at warm temperatures (the Dung Bed or slow Athanor) creates conditions that can promote anaerobic bacterial growth, including in some circumstances organisms capable of producing toxic metabolites.
- **Dew collection** gathers atmospheric and surface material that may include fungal spores, pollen, bird droppings, insect material, and environmental pollutants, depending on collection location and season.
- **Rainwater collection** — particularly first-flush water or water collected near trees, roofing, or soil — may contain elevated biological and chemical load.
- **Extended maceration** in partially sealed or sealed vessels at room temperature may produce unexpected fermentation or mould growth, particularly in warm months.

None of these hazards is exotic or alarming when approached with standard hygienic practice. The following protocols are designed to make that practice explicit.

### Dew Collection (Gur) — Safety Protocols

**Collection site:** Collect only from surfaces that are clean, uncontaminated by agricultural chemicals, vehicle exhaust, heavy foot traffic, or animal use. A clean lawn, meadow, or garden away from roads is appropriate. Urban rooftops or gutters are not appropriate collection sites.

**Time and season:** Collect at first light, before temperature rises significantly. Spring and autumn dews are cooler and less biologically active than summer dews. Do not collect dew following a long dry spell (concentration of surface contaminants) or following storm activity.

**Collection vessels:** Use clean, glass or food-grade stainless vessels only. Wash with hot water and allow to dry fully before collection. Do not use plastic containers that may leach.

**Handling:** Handle collected dew using nitrile gloves. Do not splash into eyes. Do not drink raw dew.

**Processing:** Dew intended for laboratory use should be filtered through a clean paper filter before use. Dew intended for extended maceration should be used promptly or refrigerated (below 4°C) if not used the same day.

**If you are immunocompromised, pregnant, or have a respiratory condition:** Discuss with your Guide before engaging in dew collection. Exposure to concentrated fungal spores or biological material in dews may present elevated risk. Consult your medical practitioner.

### Rainwater Collection — Safety Protocols

**First-flush rule:** Discard the first 10–15 minutes of rainfall. The initial rain carries the highest load of atmospheric particulates, surface contamination, and biological material.

**Collection surface:** Collect from a clean surface — glass, glazed ceramic, or food-grade stainless. Do not collect from galvanised, lead-flashed, or tarred roofing.

**Filtration:** Filter collected rainwater through a clean paper filter before any laboratory use.

**Storage:** Store filtered rainwater in sealed glass containers in a cool, dark location. Use within two weeks, or refrigerate.

### Putrefaction Work — Safety Protocols

Putrefaction (the Black Stage, Nigredo) is a controlled decomposition process conducted in a sealed vessel over heat. The following precautions apply.

**Vessel integrity:** Use only undamaged, properly sealed glass vessels. Inspect before each use. A cracked vessel that develops pressure from fermentation gases can fail unexpectedly.

**Opening sealed vessels:** Open slowly, away from face, and in a ventilated space. Allow gas pressure to equalise before fully removing the stopper. **Never heat a completely sealed, rigid vessel.** Fermentation and thermal expansion can build dangerous internal pressure.

**Odour:** Putrefaction produces strong odours — sulfurous, ammoniacal, or simply strongly organic. These odours are unpleasant and should be taken seriously as a ventilation cue, not merely endured. Ensure adequate ventilation whenever opening putrefying vessels.

**Mould growth:** Visible mould growth (surface growth on plant matter in maceration) is expected in some preparations and represents early-stage Nigredo. However:
- Do not handle mouldy materials with bare hands. Use gloves.
- Do not inhale directly over an open vessel containing visible mould growth. Hold breath briefly, or turn face away, when opening.
- If you have known mould sensitivity or asthma, take additional precautions and inform your Guide.
- Extensive mould growth that produces coloured spores (blue-green *Penicillium*, black *Aspergillus*) should be noted in your journal and reported to your Guide. These moulds are generally not dangerous to healthy adults in small-vessel quantities but warrant awareness.

**Unexpected fermentation:** If a vessel shows unexpected vigorous fermentation (bubbling, pressure, unusual smell), do not open until activity has subsided. Allow the vessel to equilibrate at room temperature. If activity does not subside within 24 hours, or if the vessel shows signs of stress, report to your Guide before proceeding.

### General Hygiene Protocols — Wet Path Work

- **Wash hands** thoroughly before and after all wet path sessions.
- **Dedicated equipment:** Use separate equipment for wet path biological work and for mineral work. Do not cross-contaminate.
- **Cleaning vessels:** Wash all vessels used in putrefaction or extended maceration with hot water and a biodegradable laboratory detergent. Rinse thoroughly. Air-dry fully before storage.
- **Waste disposal:** Spent putrefied plant material is organic waste. It may be composted if it contains no harmful additives. Do not pour concentrated fermented liquids in large quantities directly into waterways.
- **Record-keeping:** Note the date, material, condition, and any unusual observations (unexpected colour, odour, growth) for every wet path session.

---

### Cross-Reference — Ventilation and PPE

Biological safety in wet path work intersects with the general laboratory safety requirements of Safety Document E.1. At minimum:

- Ventilate whenever opening putrefying vessels.
- Use nitrile gloves for all handling of putrefying or moulded material.
- Avoid touching face during sessions.

The higher-level PPE requirements of E.3 (respirator, splash goggles) are not required for standard plant putrefaction work under normal conditions, but students with respiratory sensitivities may choose to use an N95 when opening vessels with heavy mould growth.

---

### When to Pause and Consult

Stop wet path work and contact your Guide if:
- You develop an unexpected illness (gastroenteritis, respiratory symptoms, skin reaction) following a wet path session
- A vessel shows behaviour (extreme pressure, unusual colour change, unexpected violent fermentation) that you cannot account for from the curriculum material
- You are pregnant or become pregnant during the programme (discuss the full scope of biological material exposure with your medical practitioner)
- Your immune status changes during the programme in a way that may affect your response to biological exposure

In any genuine health emergency, contact appropriate medical services directly. Do not wait for a Guide response.
